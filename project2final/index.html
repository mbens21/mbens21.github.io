About the Website

This website has information on the 10 most visited national parks in 2018 according to the National Park Service's website. The goal of the project is to create a fun, yet informational platform for middle schoolers to learn about the parks and then test their knowledge. Click a button on the map to start learning!

Glacier

  • President William Taft signed a bill into law establishing the park on May 11, 1910
  • Glacier straddles the Continental Divide, where opposing Pacific and Arctic airs meet, creating extreme weather
  • Joins and colloborates with Waterton Lakes National Park in Alberton, Canada, to form the world's first international peace park
×

Olympic

  • Designated a national park by President Franklin Roosevelt on June 29, 1938
  • There are several distinctly different ecosystems, including glacier-capped mountains, temperate rain forests, and over 70 miles of wild coastline
  • Endemic species like the Olympic marmot, Olympic snow mole, and Olympic torrent salamander are found here and nowhere else
×

Yellowstone

  • Congress established it as the first national park on March 1, 1872
  • Preserves more than 10,000 hydrothermal features including hot springs, mudpots, fumaroles, travertine terraces, and geysers
  • Beneath the park lies one of the world’s largest active volcanoes and the first major eruption occurred 2.1 million years ago
×

Yosemite

  • Established as a national park by Congress on October 14, 1890
  • Known for its waterfalls, including 2,425-foot Yosemite Falls that ranks as the tallest in North America, flowing down into the Valley meadows
  • Granite rock formations such as El Capitan and Half Dome are illuminated in reds and oranges at sunset, appearing to be on fire
×

Grand Teton

  • Established in 1929 by President Coolidge and merged with Jackson Hole National Monument in 1950 under President Truman
  • Coldest temperature ever recorded in the park was -63°F, and snow often blankets the landscape from early November to May
  • Earthquakes created the 40-mile-long Teton Range, which sits on a fault line
×

Zion

  • Presdient Woodrow Wilson signed a bill into law establishing Zion National Park on November 19, 1919
  • Zion-Mt. Carmel Highway and Tunnel is a 1.1-mile-long tunnel cutting through vertical sandstone cliffs of Zion
  • Zion's 2,000-foot sandstone cliffs are world renowned for their big wall climbs
×

Grand Canyon

  • President Woodrow Wilson signed a bill into law estblishing Grand Canyon National Park on February 26, 1919
  • Tucked within the Grand Canyon are an estimated 1,000 caves, 335 that have been recorded, and one that is open to the public
  • Grand Canyon is bigger than Rhode Island and not all of it is included in the park
×

Rocky Mountain

  • President Woodrow Wilson signed the Rocky Mountain National Park Act on January 26, 1915
  • One of the nation’s highest national parks, with elevations from 7,860 feet to 14,259 feet and 77 mountain peaks over 12,000 feet high
  • About one-third of the park is above the limit where trees grow in northern Colorado, creating the alpine tundra ecosystem
×

Great Smoky Mountains

  • Established on June 15, 1934, but President Franklin D. Roosevelt formally dedicated the park on September 2, 1940
  • Most visited national park, renowned for its plant and animal life, mountains, and remnants of Southern Appalachian mountain culture
  • Biologists estimate that roughly 1,500 bears live in the park and inhabit all elevations
×

Acadia

  • Established as Sieur de Monts National Monument in July 1916, changed to Lafayette National Park in February 1919, and named Acadia National Park in January 1929
  • Thunder Hole is a semi-submerged cave where crashing waves sound like thunder
  • 1,530-foot-high Cadillac Mountain is the tallest point on the eastern coast
